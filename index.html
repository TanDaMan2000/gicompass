The problem

Digestive symptoms are easy to ignore. That is the problem.

Early-onset colorectal cancer can be missed because initial symptoms may look vague, intermittent, or easy to explain away. Changes in bowel habits, rectal bleeding, abdominal pain, fatigue, and other symptoms do not always trigger the right level of concern early enough. GI Compass helps users track what they are feeling in one place so persistent symptom patterns are easier to catch and escalate sooner.

The solution

A sharper way to flag symptom patterns that should not be ignored

Symptom Tracking

Patients can log rectal bleeding, bowel changes, abdominal pain, fatigue, weight loss, and symptom duration in seconds.

Risk Context Intake

Age, sex, ethnicity, family history of colorectal cancer, and prior colonoscopy status help frame the symptom pattern more realistically.

Dual-View Summaries

Turn messy symptom history into one organized summary that patients and clinicians can review more clearly.

Early Insight

Highlight symptom patterns that may warrant earlier physician follow-up instead of continued dismissal or delay.

Why the approach is different

Designed for a documented failure mode in primary care

Symptoms that are present, but ignored

Early-onset colorectal cancer is one of the few GI problems where warning symptoms often exist but are written off as IBS, hemorrhoids, stress, or anxiety.

More than a symptom tracker

GI Compass is not trying to broadly classify “GI disease.” It is being shaped as a focused escalation tool for under-50 patients whose symptom patterns deserve attention sooner.

Imaging is a roadmap feature

The imaging layer remains future work until it can be trained and validated on clinically meaningful data. The symptom-side is the tractable first build.

Focused clinical direction

Starting with early-onset colorectal cancer

GI Compass is being shaped around a narrower, clinically meaningful question: can a symptom-first risk flagging system help identify under-50 patients whose warning signs for colorectal cancer are being overlooked? That focus is stronger because it addresses a real and documented failure mode, instead of trying to cover a broad and noisy GI category.

The goal is earlier recognition and better escalation, not diagnosis inside the app.

Why this matters

Early symptoms are often the first signal that something is wrong, but they are easy to minimize when they happen across days or weeks. GI Compass is focused on the cases where symptoms are present and repeatedly dismissed. The goal is not to diagnose. FAQ

Common questions

Is GI Compass a diagnosis tool?

No. GI Compass is meant to help users organize symptoms and notice patterns, not diagnose or replace medical care.

What symptoms can I track?

You can track rectal bleeding, bowel habit changes, abdominal pain, fatigue, weight change, timing, and other symptom notes.

What makes GI Compass different from a regular symptom tracker?

GI Compass is being built around a specific clinical problem: under-50 colorectal cancer symptoms that are present but dismissed. It is an escalation support tool, not a general-purpose GI diary.

Who is this app for?

It is built for teens, adults, parents, and anyone trying to understand ongoing digestive symptoms more clearly.

Can I share my results with a doctor?

Yes. GI Compass is designed to make it easier to export concise summaries and bring them into appointments.

What condition is GI Compass focused on first?

The current product direction is focused on early-onset colorectal cancer, where earlier symptom pattern recognition could support faster evaluation and clearer clinical action.

Is the imaging component live today?

No. The imaging layer is a roadmap feature and will only make sense once it can be trained and validated on clinically meaningful data. The symptom-side is the first build.

Is my health data private?

The product vision prioritizes privacy, secure handling, and transparent use of health information.
