The problem

Digestive symptoms are easy to ignore. That is the problem.

Early-onset colorectal cancer can be missed because initial symptoms may look vague, intermittent, or easy to explain away. Changes in bowel habits, rectal bleeding, abdominal pain, fatigue, and other symptoms do not always trigger the right level of concern early enough. Gastro Compass is focused on that failure mode, and GastroLens is being built to turn the work into a clearer, symptom-first prototype workflow for earlier escalation support.

What GastroLens is being designed to do

A sharper upcoming workflow for symptom patterns that should not be ignored

Symptom Tracking

The prototype is being designed so patients can log rectal bleeding, bowel changes, abdominal pain, fatigue, weight loss, and symptom duration in seconds.

Risk Context Intake

Age, sex, ethnicity, family history of colorectal cancer, and prior colonoscopy status help frame the symptom pattern more realistically.

Dual-View Summaries

Turn messy symptom history into one organized prototype summary that patients and clinicians could review more clearly.

Early Insight

Highlight symptom patterns that may warrant earlier physician follow-up instead of continued dismissal or delay.

Why the approach is different

Designed around a documented failure mode, with research first

Symptoms that are present, but ignored

Early-onset colorectal cancer is one of the few GI problems where warning symptoms often exist but are written off as IBS, hemorrhoids, stress, or anxiety.

Built from completed research

Gastro Compass brings together the completed paper, prototype design process, NHANES-grounded feasibility work, and three empirical analyses behind the concept.

Imaging is a roadmap feature

The imaging layer remains future work until it can be trained and validated on clinically meaningful data. The symptom-side is the tractable first build.

Safety-first framing

GastroLens is being positioned as an escalation support prototype, not a diagnosis tool. The product direction stays non-clinical until it is properly validated.

GastroLens is the upcoming interactive prototype built from the Gastro Compass research hub. This preview shows the planned workflow, why it matters, and how the concept is intended to support follow-up without implying public availability.

1

Capture

Patients would log symptoms such as rectal bleeding, bowel changes, abdominal pain, fatigue, and weight loss over time.

2

Organize

The prototype would turn daily symptom entries into a clearer timeline instead of leaving important details scattered across memory and notes.

3

Analyze

The planned system would weigh persistence, recurrence, and risk context like age, ethnicity, family history, and prior colonoscopy status.

Escalate

The intended output is a summary that says the pattern warrants a conversation with a physician, not a diagnosis inside the prototype.

FAQ

Common questions

What is Gastro Compass?

Gastro Compass is the umbrella project building GastroLens. It brings together the concept, research framing, prototype previews, and early access updates in one place.

Is GastroLens available to the public yet?

No. GastroLens is coming soon. The current site is previewing the concept and workflow while the prototype remains in active development.

What will GastroLens do?

GastroLens is being designed as a symptom-first GI risk flagging prototype that helps organize persistent warning signs, risk context, and follow-up-ready summaries for under-50 colorectal cancer concern.

Are the dashboard tabs live product features?

No. The Patient View, Professional View, AI Review, and Exports tabs are prototype previews that show interface directions in development.

Is GastroLens a diagnosis tool?

No. GastroLens is being framed as a non-diagnostic escalation support prototype. It is not clinically validated and is not meant to replace professional medical care.

Why focus on early-onset colorectal cancer first?

The research direction is focused on the documented failure mode where symptoms are present in under-50 patients but repeatedly dismissed, making symptom-first risk flagging more clinically meaningful than a broad GI classifier.

Is the imaging component live today?

No. Imaging remains a roadmap feature and will only make sense once it can be trained and validated on clinically meaningful data. The symptom-side is the first build.
